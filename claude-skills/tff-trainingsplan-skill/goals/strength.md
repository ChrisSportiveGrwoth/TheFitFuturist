## How to use this file

These are principles, not templates. Use them to reason about the user's specific situation and derive the optimal plan. The goal distinction (maximal strength vs. hypertrophy) matters — clarify it before planning.

---

## Principle 1: Goal Distinction

**Ask if unclear — or infer from assessment.**

| Goal | Primary driver | Rep range | Load | Rest |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Maximal strength | Neuromuscular adaptation | 1-5 | Heavy (85-100% 1RM) | 3-5 min |
| Hypertrophy | Mechanical tension + volume | 6-15 | Moderate-heavy (65-85% 1RM) | 2-3 min |
| General fitness | Both, conservatively | 8-12 | Moderate | 90-120 sec |

**Note on rest periods:** Longer rest (2-3 min) is now supported for hypertrophy — it allows more total work per session by maintaining rep quality. Shorter rest (60-90 sec) increases metabolic stress but reduces per-set quality; use for isolation exercises or when time-constrained.

Mixed goals are possible — prioritize based on user's primary stated motivation.

## Principle 2: Progressive Overload

**The principle:**
The training stimulus must increase over time for adaptation to continue. Without progression, the body has no reason to change.

**Evidence:**
- Volume (sets × reps × load) is the primary driver of hypertrophy
- Load progression and rep progression are equally effective for hypertrophy — both are valid
- For maximal strength: load progression is superior to rep progression
- Volume has diminishing returns — more is better up to a point, then recovery suffers

**How to apply:**
- Beginners: linear progression — increase load every session (the body adapts quickly)
- Intermediate: weekly progression — increase load or reps each week
- Advanced: block/wave periodization — progression happens over 3-6 week cycles

**Standard progression method — Double Progression (use for hypertrophy and general fitness):**

Use a rep range per exercise (e.g. 8–12 reps). Progress reps first, then load:
1. Train with the same weight until **sets 1 AND 2** both reach the top of the range with good form
2. Then increase load by ~5–10% (not a fixed kg amount — percentages account for the difference between dumbbell curls and leg press)
3. After a load increase, expect to drop back toward the bottom of the range — that is correct and expected
4. Natural rep drop across sets is normal and intended: e.g. Set 1: 12 / Set 2: 11 / Set 3: 8 — this reflects fatigue accumulation, not failure

**Why not fixed kg increments (+2.5kg):**
+2.5kg on a curl is a 15–20% jump. +2.5kg on a leg press is 2–3%. Percentage-based increases are self-scaling and appropriate across all exercises.

**For maximal strength goals:** load progression is primary — add weight when the target reps are achieved with RIR ≥ 2.

**Other progression options (secondary — use when load increase is not yet appropriate):**
- Add reps within range (same load)
- Add a set per exercise per week
- Reduce rest time (same load and reps — increases metabolic demand)

Never increase load AND volume simultaneously in the same week.

## Principle 3: Volume and Frequency

**The principle:**
Weekly sets per muscle group determine hypertrophy outcomes. Frequency determines how those sets are distributed.

**Evidence-based targets:**
- Minimum effective dose: 4-6 sets/muscle/week
- Optimal range: 10-20 sets/muscle/week (population average — individual variation is significant)
- Diminishing returns beyond 20 sets/muscle/week for most people
- **Monitor individual response:** some users plateau at 12 sets, others handle 22+ before recovery suffers. Use performance and fatigue signals to find the effective range for each user — not just the population average.

**Frequency:**
- 2x per week per muscle group: evidence-based standard for both strength and hypertrophy
- Beginners: 2–3x/week is sufficient and optimal. 4x is fine if the user wants it — just use lower per-session volume and appropriate exercise complexity. Do not push beginners toward 3x if they want 4x.
- Intermediate+: 4-day splits (upper/lower or PPL) achieve 2x frequency efficiently

**Frequency reasoning for planning:**
Derive the split from available days:
- 2-3 days → Full body (each session hits all major muscle groups)
- 4 days → Upper/Lower split (each muscle 2x/week)
- 5+ days → PPL or specialized split

Never recommend a split that hits each muscle only 1x/week (bro split) for hypertrophy goals — evidence does not support it.

**Age-adjusted frequency (apply based on Block 2 age input):**

| Age | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| <40 | Standard frequency — 3x/week per muscle group fully appropriate |
| 40–49 | Standard frequency still appropriate. Allow 72h between heavy compound sessions if fatigue signals appear. No mandatory changes. |
| 50–59 | Recommend 2–3x/week; heavy compound movements (squat, deadlift) better at 2x. Deload every 3–4 weeks instead of 4–6. Explicitly mention connective tissue needs more recovery than muscle. |
| 60–69 | Recommend 2x/week per muscle group as primary. Volume per session more important than frequency. Prioritize eccentric loading and full ROM. Deload every 3 weeks. |
| 70+ | 2x/week minimum for sarcopenia prevention. Lower intensity per session, higher frequency of lighter sessions acceptable. Mention doctor consultation alongside plan. |

Note: Muscle protein synthesis rate is similar across ages — the key difference is recovery duration, not anabolic capacity (Tipton 2015, Peterson et al. 2011). Older trainees need more time between sessions, not less training.

## Principle 4: Proximity to Failure

**The principle:**
Sets taken close to failure produce more hypertrophic stimulus than comfortable sets. But failure every set accumulates fatigue rapidly.

**Evidence-based approach — compound vs. isolation distinction:**
- **Compound movements** (squat, deadlift, bench, row): RIR 3-5 is optimal. Training closer to failure adds fatigue without proportional hypertrophic benefit and increases injury risk. Recent meta-analyses (Lasevicius 2022, Refalo 2022) confirm similar hypertrophy across RIR 1-6 when volume is equated.
- **Isolation exercises** (curl, lateral raise, leg extension): RIR 1-3 — closer to failure is more important here; less CNS fatigue, easier to recover from.
- Beginners: RIR 4-6 on all movements — priority is movement pattern learning, not proximity to failure.
- Advanced: occasional true failure sets on isolation work acceptable; avoid on compounds.

**RPE scale for planning:**
- RPE 6: Could do 4+ more reps — warm-up only
- RPE 7: Could do 3 more reps
- RPE 8: Could do 2 more reps — most working sets
- RPE 9: Could do 1 more rep — occasional
- RPE 10: True failure — use sparingly

## Principle 5: Exercise Selection

**The principle:**
Compound movements build the most muscle and strength per unit of training time. Isolation work adds volume to specific muscles when needed.

**Minimum Exercise Anchors — required in every strength plan (all sessions combined):**
- **Eccentric lower-leg:** Single-leg calf raise with 3s lowering — must appear in the FIRST lower-body or full-body session of Week 1
- **Posterior chain:** Nordic curl / sofa curl or RDL — must appear in the FIRST lower-body or full-body session of Week 1
- **Hip stability:** Clamshell or side-lying abduction — include in at least one session per week

**Reasoning hierarchy:**
1. Choose compound movements as the foundation (squat, deadlift, bench, row, overhead press)
2. Add isolation work for lagging muscle groups or specific goals
3. Select based on available equipment (see below)

**Full ROM principle:**
Full range of motion produces greater hypertrophy than partial ROM — particularly because loading the muscle at its lengthened position (e.g., bottom of a squat, bottom of a Romanian deadlift, full stretch on a curl) generates more mechanical tension. Prioritize exercises that allow a full stretch: deep squat, full-range RDL, incline curl, overhead tricep extension. Partial ROM is only appropriate as a training variation, not a default.

**Movement pattern complexity — use assessment to gate exercise selection:**

| Movement pattern familiarity | Exercise selection rule |
|---|---|
| Pattern unknown | Machine or dumbbell substitute only — no barbell, no complex setup. Example: hinge unknown → leg press or cable pull-through instead of RDL |
| Pattern known, beginner level | Include with form cue. Use simpler loaded variation first (goblet squat before barbell squat, DB RDL before barbell RDL) |
| Pattern known, intermediate+ | Full compound selection appropriate |

Never assign two hinge-pattern exercises (e.g. RDL + stiff-leg deadlift) to a beginner in the same session — one hinge movement per session maximum until movement quality is confirmed.

**Equipment reasoning:**
- Full gym: Barbell compound movements as primary. Dumbbell and cable as accessory.
- Home gym (dumbbells): DB bench, DB row, DB RDL as primary. Add bands for pulldowns/face pulls.
- Bodyweight only: Derive progressions — push-up → archer → one-arm, squat → pistol, row → Australian. These are real strength progressions, not compromises.
- Outdoor only: Calisthenics program using park bars and bodyweight. Hill sprints for lower body power.

## Principle 6: Recovery

**The principle:**
Muscle adaptation happens during recovery, not during training. Insufficient recovery negates the training stimulus.

**Recovery times:**
| Session intensity | Age <50 | Age 50+ |
|---|---|---|
| Heavy compound (RPE 8-9) | 48–72h | 72–96h |
| Moderate hypertrophy (RPE 7-8) | 48h | 48–72h |
| Light / technique | 24h | 24–48h |

**Deload triggers:**
- Every 4-6 weeks: planned deload — reduce volume 40-50%; if accumulated fatigue is high, also reduce load 10-15% to allow neural recovery (pure volume reduction without load reduction is less effective after high-intensity strength blocks)
- Unplanned deload triggers: stalled progress for 2+ weeks, persistent joint pain, poor sleep quality, motivation significantly reduced

## Principle 7: Red Flags — Adapt or Stop

| Signal | Reasoning | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Joint pain (not muscle soreness) | Possible overuse or injury | Remove the movement, do not push through |
| Lower back pain during squat/deadlift | Technical breakdown or overload | Reduce load significantly, check form cues |
| Shoulder pain during pressing | Rotator cuff or impingement | Check grip width, add face pulls and external rotation work |
| Strength plateau >3 weeks | Adaptation stall | Deload, then change rep range or split |
| Persistent fatigue | Overtraining or under-recovery | Deload immediately, assess sleep and nutrition |
